Japan

Why Japan is Special

Japan is not merely a country; it's a vibrant tapestry of experiences that engage all the senses. It embodies the essence of a cyberpunk fantasy, where time-honored traditions meet state-of-the-art technology. Picture peaceful temples nestled within lively urban areas, neon-lit entertainment hubs adjacent to serene gardens, and futuristic robot restaurants located just a stone's throw from Michelin-starred sushi establishments. This delightful and exhilarating blend of contrasts is what makes Japan truly unique.

How to Get There

  • International Flights: You'll most likely fly into Narita International Airport (NRT) or Haneda Airport (HND). Haneda is closer to the major urban areas and generally preferred.
  • From the Airports to Japan:
  • Narita: The Narita Express (N'EX) is the fastest train to various major cities, including Tokyo. The Keisei Skyliner is another good option. Airport limousine buses are also available.
  • Haneda: The Keikyu Line and the Tokyo Monorail are both efficient train options for reaching central locations. Limousine buses are also readily available.
  • Within Japan: Japan's public transportation system is a marvel. The JR Yamanote Line, the Tokyo Metro, and the Toei Subway are your best friends in the capital. For travel across the country, consider using the Shinkansen (bullet train) for fast and convenient connections. Get a Suica or Pasmo card for easy travel in urban areas.

Other Places to Visit in Japan:

Japan itself is a universe, but here's a quick hit list:

  • Sensō-ji It is an ancient Buddhist temple in Asakusa, Tokyo, Japan. It is Tokyo's oldest-established temple, and one of its most significant. It is dedicated to Kannon, the bodhisattva of compassion. Structures in the temple complex include the main hall, a five-story pagoda and large gates.
  • Shinjuku City Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building (free panoramic views), Golden Gai (tiny, atmospheric bars), and Shinjuku Gyoen National Garden.
  • Mt. Fuji It is an active volcano about 100 kilometers southwest of Tokyo. Commonly called “Fuji-san,” it’s the country’s tallest peak, at 3,776 meters. A pilgrimage site for centuries, it’s considered one of Japan’s 3 sacred mountains, and summit hikes remain a popular activity. Its iconic profile is the subject of numerous works of art, notably Edo Period prints by Hokusai and Hiroshige.
  • Asakusa: Senso-ji Temple, traditional street food, and the Sumida River cruise.
  • Harajuku: Takeshita Street (crazy fashion), Meiji Jingu Shrine (peaceful oasis).
  • Ueno Park: Museums, zoo, and a lovely green space.
  • Ghibli Museum (Mitaka): If you can score tickets, it's a must-see for Ghibli fans.
